  • Wall Street Journal

    "China's Yuan Tumbles on Speculation Over Central Bank Move Soon"
    2015

      Content
    • Two different exchange rates based on where it's traded: offshore or on mainland China. Traders watch the offshore rate because it is more open to foreign investors and less subject to state control.
    • When offshore rate trades below the mainland rate, typically this indicates a weaker overseas demand for the currency. Can also be a sign investors believe the Chinese government will lower the official exchange rate.
    • The People's Bank of China sets a daily reference rate for the yuan, then allows it to trade 2% above or below that level.
    • The central bank surprised the market by setting the midpoint rate for the yuan’s trading range higher despite the yuan's fall against the dollar.

      • Predictions
    • Investors expect the central bank to continue a recent trend of nudging the yuan lower against the dollar.
    • "People are betting for further depreciation" - Win Thin, global head of emerging-market currency strategy at Brown Brothers Harriman.
    • Authorities won't allow sharp yuan depreciation.
    • "I don't think they'll introduce big changes when markets are in distress," Mr. Thin said, adding that currencies in Russia and Brazil are tumbling against the dollar. "That's not the time to make currency policy changes, and I think China knows that."

  • Wall Street Journal

    "Rising Dollar Presses Asian Borrowers"
    2015

    • Companies in Southeast Asia are struggling with loans taken when China's ecomony was growing
    • As China's economy weakens, companies need more local currency to pay back their loans
    • “The biggest threat to a borrower is a foreign-exchange move against you and you have a U.S. dollar liability and your profit stream is in domestic currency,” said Keith Pogson, financial-services managing partner for the Asian- Pacific region at Ernst & Young.

  • Science Direct: Journal of International Money and Finance

    "Do external political pressures affect the Renminbi exchange rate?"
    2012

    • Authors created measures of external political pressure on the RMB exchange rate with special emphasis on US pressure
    • After accounting for macroeconomic shocks, no statistical correlation was found between political pressure and exchange rate shifts
    • It is possible that persistent external pressures on the RMB exchange could introduce market uncertainty and push the RMB appreciation in the long run.

  • China and World Economy

    "Chinese Yuan After Chinese Exchange Rate Reform"
    2006

      Predictions
    • The Balassa-Samuelson hypothesis is used to predict possible trends for the yuan
    • The Balassa-Samuelson hypothesis assumes productivity in tradable goods is increasing at a faster rate than that of non-tradable goods. As a result, wages for employees in the tradable good sector and the non-tradable goods sector must compensate by increasing prices in order to remain profitable
    • The conclusion is that the purchasing power parity of the yuan will be overvalued by about 0.7% against the dollar.
